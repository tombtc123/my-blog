You noticed a fact many people would rather not admit: honesty is not a byproduct of intelligence. Smart people are often better at packaging things, disclosing selectively, and turning lies into systems. Low-level lying is called deception. High-level lying is often called narrative, positioning, expectation management, trade secrets, or strategic ambiguity.

But there is also a dangerous slide in that thought. You have compressed the toolbox of the strong into the single word "lying." A truly strong person is not someone who habitually profits by lying. A strong person can control information, design rules, bear consequences, and knows when credit must be protected. In long games, trust is not a moral medal. It is a hard asset that lowers transaction costs. Burning trust for one payout is not necessarily strength. Very often, it is just a more vicious version of short-sightedness.

Shame is not reserved for naive people either. Shame is internal risk control. The question is not whether you feel shame when you lie. The question is whether you can distinguish what should remain confidential, what is negotiation strategy, what is lawful strategic ambiguity, and what is fraud. If someone cannot separate those four things and starts worshiping lies, that is not becoming darker. That is becoming smaller.

The distinction worth remembering is this: not telling the truth and telling a falsehood are not the same thing. Silence, refusal to answer, delayed commitment, and disclosing only necessary information are adult ways to protect boundaries. Fabricating facts and leading someone to hand over value based on false information belong to a different cost system. Legal costs, reputational costs, retaliation costs, and the psychological cost of maintaining the lie will eventually come back.

So this idea should be kept, but only halfway. Keep the part that says: do not be naive enough to think powerful people move through the world on sincerity alone. Cut the part that says: once you learn to lie, you are standing with the strong. Strong people are not strong because they lie. They are strong because they know when to use truth, falsehood, silence, contracts, leverage, and exit rights.

One sentence for you: when shame stops you from protecting yourself, you are naive; when you make yourself dirty just to prove you are no longer naive, you are still naive.
